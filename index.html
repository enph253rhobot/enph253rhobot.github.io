About the Competition

Every year, the UBC Engineering Physics department runs a rapid prototyping course for students finishing their second year. Students are divided into teams of four, and spend about 6 weeks designing and building a robot to accomplish some task. At the end of the course, a competition is held between teams in the class. For the summer of 2016, the competition surface was a miniature 8 x 16 foot city, with roads as black electrical tape, and stuffed animal passengers distributed around the city. The goal of the competition was to navigate the city and collect passengers along the way. Once a passenger is collected, the robot was to bring it to the destination for drop-off. One of the main challenges of the competition was to be racing against an opposing robot to collect the most passengers within a 2 minute time frame. See Figure 1 for a visualization. The dotted lines in Figure 1 denote city blocks, the red lines denote black electrical tape (roads), and solid blue lines denote competition surface boundaries.

Mechanical Design

For the mechanical aspects of our robot, we designed the main chassis, arm and claw mechanism, and the drivetrain.

Electrical Design

For the electrical aspects of our robot, we designed circuitry for tape-following, IR detection, and motor driving.

Software Design

For the software aspects of our robot, we wrote algorithms for navigating the competition surface, picking up and dropping off passengers, and PID control for tape-following.

  • Arm & Claw

    A 3-axis parallel arm was created to reach out for passengers at various distances, with a claw shaped perfectly for the passenger size.

    Arm & Claw Details

  • Tape-Following

    QRD1114 Optical Detectors were used in a comparator circuit to determine when the robot was on or off the tape. PID controls were implemented for self-correcting and smooth tape-following maneuvers.

    Tape-Following Details

  • IR Detection

    QSD124 Phototransistors were used in with biquad circuits to bandpass filter for 1kHz IR, amplify, and peak detect the signal from passengers and the dropoff zone. IR Sensors were used to faciliate navigation, pickup, and dropoff.

    IR Detection Details

  • Navigation

    The competition map was stored in our robot, allowing it to calculate the available paths to traverse when seeking passengers and the optimal path to the dropoff once a passenger was acquired.
